The problem

Fired heaters burn the most fuel on a refinery.

A refinery runs dozens of fired heaters. Each one burns fuel gas to raise a process stream to temperature, and each is the largest single energy user and CO₂ source on its unit.

Operators hold extra combustion air in the firebox as a safety margin against incomplete burning. That margin is expensive. Every extra percent of air carries heat up the stack and raises the fuel bill.

Run the air too rich and you waste fuel. Run it too lean and you make carbon monoxide. The safe optimum keeps moving with fuel composition, throughput, fouling and weather.

No operator can track that optimum by hand across a shift. A model can, if it understands the combustion physics.

The wedge

Start from physics, then learn from the plant.

Most digital twins need months of clean plant history before they predict anything. This one does not.

We build the heater from first principles: combustion chemistry, mass and energy balance, heat transfer. The model is useful on the first day of a deployment, with no historian required. This solves the cold-start problem that stalls most industrial machine-learning projects.

Once the plant historian is connected, the same model calibrates to the real unit and keeps improving. Physics gives the head start, plant data gives the accuracy.

How it works

Seven steps from raw physics to a live recommendation.

The prototype in this deck runs the full sequence end to end.

01
Collect
First-principles simulation stands in for the plant historian at ideation stage.
02
Clean
Faulty sensor tags detected and removed. Real process excursions kept.
03
Label
Seven fault classes labelled as ground truth for supervised learning.
04
Model
A surrogate learns heater response from controls and disturbances.
05
Train
Fit on 2,081 rows, held out on 694 the model never saw.
06
Validate
Predicted versus truth, scored per target. R² 0.96 to 0.99.
07
Deploy
Live dashboard reads KPIs and recommends the optimal setpoint.

A single refinery runs 20 to 100 of these heaters, so the site opportunity runs into the millions of dollars a year. The same model class extends to boilers, crackers and reformers, which widens the market well beyond the first use case.

Assumptions: fuel gas at 6 USD/GJ, carbon at 40 USD/t CO₂, 8,400 operating hours per year, carbon monoxide limit 200 ppm. Change any input and the twin recomputes the recommendation live.
